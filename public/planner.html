Your application timeline

The timing playbook.

Plain-language guidance for sequencing the science core, the MCAT, and the application itself.

The "minus two" rule

Medical school applications open in early May, two years before you'd matriculate. So if you want to start med school in Fall 2028, you apply in Summer 2027, which means your MCAT score should be in hand by roughly April–June 2027. Working backwards from there is the entire game.

"Apply early" is not a slogan — it's the single biggest factor under your control. Rolling admissions means a complete July application is meaningfully stronger than the same application submitted in October.

What to finish before MCAT day

The exam covers four sections: Bio/Biochem, Chem/Phys, Psych/Soc, and CARS (critical reading). To not be guessing your way through content, finish:

  • General chemistry (both semesters) and organic chemistry I — minimum
  • Introductory biology (both semesters)
  • Biochemistry — non-negotiable, this is roughly 25% of the exam
  • Introductory psychology and sociology — easy points if covered
  • Physics I and II — physics shows up across both science sections

Organic II is helpful but not strictly required for the MCAT itself. Many students take it after the test.

Study runways that actually work

Most successful students do 3–4 months of dedicated study, somewhere between 300 and 500 hours total. Two common patterns:

  • The summer sprint: 8–10 weeks at 40+ hours/week, taken between sophomore and junior summer or the summer before applying. Intense but contained.
  • The split runway: Light content review during a spring semester (10–15 hrs/week), then a 2-month full-time push into a May/June test date.

If you're juggling a full course load and meaningful clinical hours, the split runway tends to fail less often.

The traditional vs. gap-year fork

Traditional path (matriculate right after senior year): MCAT by spring of junior year, apply that summer. This requires being deep into the science sequence by the end of sophomore year — biochem usually means front-loading orgo.

One or two gap years (the more common path now — over 70% of matriculants take at least one): MCAT in the summer or fall after junior year, or even after graduating. More room for clinical hours, research, and a polished application. Schools do not penalize gap years; many actively prefer the maturity.

Things often missed by sophomores

  • UNC and ECU don't accept AP credit for prerequisites — you'll need to take the actual courses or upper-level replacements
  • ECU requires 5-year-fresh science coursework (measured from your graduation date)
  • Most schools want at least some clinical patient-facing hours; ECU expects them at the time of application
  • UNC requires two upper-level biology courses plus biochemistry — not just the intro sequence
  • Statistics is required or recommended at most schools and is increasingly common as a standalone prereq

MD vs. DO — and why DO belongs on your list

DO programs (Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine) graduate physicians who practice identically to MDs across all 50 states — same residencies, same licensure, same scope of practice. The only meaningful differences for an applicant: DO schools place additional emphasis on osteopathic manipulative medicine in their curriculum, use AACOMAS instead of AMCAS, and on average accept students with lower stats (national DO average MCAT ~503, GPA ~3.60 vs. MD ~512, ~3.79).

For a balanced school list, pre-health advisors increasingly recommend including 4–8 DO programs alongside your MD applications, especially if your stats sit at or below the MD national averages. A DO acceptance is not a consolation prize — it's a real path to any specialty you want, and matriculants generally have stronger primary-care match outcomes than DO stats alone would suggest.

One caveat: a small number of highly competitive specialties (some surgical subspecialties, dermatology, top-tier academic radiology) historically favor MD applicants. If you're certain about one of those, weight your list toward MD. For everything else, DO is functionally equivalent.
